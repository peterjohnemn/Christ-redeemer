About the Statue

Christ the Redeemer is one of the most iconic landmarks in the world, standing tall above the city of Rio de Janeiro, Brazil. The statue represents peace, faith, and unity — serving as a beacon of hope to millions of visitors each year. Recognized as one of the New Seven Wonders of the World, it continues to inspire awe through its magnificent design and breathtaking view from Corcovado Mountain, symbolizing the enduring spirit and warmth of the Brazilian people.

Cultural Significance

Christ the Redeemer holds deep cultural and spiritual importance not only for Brazil but for people around the world. It stands as a universal symbol of peace, acceptance, and faith, overlooking Rio de Janeiro with open arms that welcome all. The statue has become a powerful emblem of Brazilian identity — blending art, religion, and national pride. Its recognition as one of the New Seven Wonders of the World highlights its global impact and the timeless message of unity it represents.

How and Why It Was Built

The statue was built to symbolize peace, faith, and the deep Christian devotion of the Brazilian people. The idea for the monument was first proposed in the 1850s, but it was revived in the early 20th century to commemorate the 100th anniversary of Brazil’s independence. Construction began in 1922 and was completed in 1931 after nine years of work. Designed by Brazilian engineer Heitor da Silva Costa and sculpted by French artist Paul Landowski, the statue was constructed using reinforced concrete and covered with thousands of soapstone tiles for durability and elegance. Standing 30 meters (98 feet) tall, with its arms stretching 28 meters (92 feet) wide, and resting on an 8-meter (26-foot) pedestal, Christ the Redeemer towers majestically atop Corcovado Mountain.
